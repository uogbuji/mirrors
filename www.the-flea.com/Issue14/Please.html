Please?

by Robert Mezey

If I swung through the jungle on a vine
And beat my chest, impatient to entwine,
Or else came on more civilized, more benign,
And knelt down at your feet or lay supine,
Making vague gestures, an ambiguous sign
Towards the general area of your shrine,
As if suggesting that you too recline,
Or even if I, by accident or design,
Let slip that love is but an anodyne,
A temporary balm, however fine,
Yes, even if I should all love resign
And let my hair grow long and swear off wine
And never write another goddamned line,
Darling, wouldn’t you be my Valentine?

 

 

14
March, 2011
Robert Mezey was professor and poet-in-residence at Pomona College 1976 to 2002. His poems and translations have appeared in many journals, including Kenyon Review, New Yorker, New Letters, Paris Review, TLS, Poetry, New Republic, Partisan Review, Harper’s, New Criterion, Hudson Review, Threepenny Review, The Dark Horse, Raritan, New Letters and National Review. He has published numerous books of verse including a Collected Poems, 1952-1999. With the late Dick Barnes he translated all of Jorge Luis Borges’ poems, of which a good many have appeared in various journals. His many awards include the Robert Frost Prize, the Stegner Fellowship and the Poets Prize (for Collected Poems). He holds an honorary degree from the World Congress of Poets and one from Kenyon College.
