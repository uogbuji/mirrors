Last Supper

by Alan Sullivan

In the house where a father lies dying,
grown-up daughters and sons
have begun surreptitiously grieving
while hospice nurses, like nuns,
murmur their grave observations:
the patient has passed beyond pain,
beyond intransigent passions
and the filial bonds that remain.

For years, when holiday suppers
gathered this clan to its seat,
the keeper of mores and manners
said grace before children could eat.
But now we are children no longer:
unsteady, a son takes the head
of a table where grandchildren hanker
to tear at the unblessed bread.

 

11
December, 2010
Alan Sullivan was legendary as a poet and as a critic of other poet’s work, especially at the AbleMuse Eratosphere Poetry Forum http://www.ablemuse.com/erato/. He wrote some extraordinarily good poetry, and his rendering into contemporary English of the Beowulf (translated with his literary partner Timothy Murphy) is, in the opinion of many, including the editor of THE FLEA, the best ever made. His translation of The Psalms (along with Seree Cohen Zohar) was finished shortly before his death, and looks set to become another classic.
